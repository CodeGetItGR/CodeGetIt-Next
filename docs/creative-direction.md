# CodeGetIt: Creative Direction

Art direction document for the CodeGetIt website redesign. Creative direction first, implementation second.
Brand system is fixed: background `#fafafa`, primary text `#0f172a`, secondary text `#475569`, accent teal `#0d9488`, display Syne, body Outfit. Light theme only.

---

## Phase 0: Diagnosis of the Industry

### The cliché inventory

Seventeen patterns that make every web-development agency site interchangeable:

1. **Floating browser/device mockups** in 3D perspective
2. **Code screenshots** with syntax highlighting
3. **Terminal windows** with fake commands typing themselves
4. **Floating dashboard cards** ("New order! +32% visitors, 4.9 stars")
5. **Glassmorphism panels** on everything
6. **Purple-blue mesh gradients** and aurora washes
7. **Generic blobs** drifting behind the headline
8. **"We build digital experiences"** and its hundred synonyms
9. **Isometric startup illustrations** (tiny people pushing giant gears)
10. **Spotlight/beam sweeps** over the hero
11. **Stat counters** (150+ projects, 99% satisfaction) counting up
12. **Flip-word headlines** cycling "websites / apps / platforms"
13. **Pulsing green "available for projects" dot**
14. **Particle networks** of connecting dots
15. **Three equal service cards** with icon, title, blurb
16. **Tech-stack logo marquees** (React, Next, Node scrolling forever)
17. **Tilt-on-hover cards** with glow borders

### Why these patterns fail

They all commit the same crime: they communicate **category membership instead of identity**. A floating dashboard card says "we are a web agency," which the visitor already knew. None of them say "we are CodeGetIt." Most are borrowed prestige from other product genres (SaaS dashboards, developer tools), so they advertise someone else's product. And because templates and AI generators mass-produce them, each one now actively signals "template," which is fatal for a studio whose entire pitch is custom work. A visitor's memory compresses the whole site into "another dev shop" and discards it within the hour.

### The Do Not Use list (binding for the whole project)

All seventeen patterns above, plus: dark-theme switches, neon accents, gradient text on large headlines, fake notifications, fake star ratings, fake screenshots built from divs, scroll-cue labels, decorative status dots, and stock photography of laptops on desks.

The audit found items 1, 4, 10, 11, 12, 13 and 17 in the current working tree. They are retired by this redesign.

## Phase 1: The Meaning of CodeGetIt

The name is a sentence. **Code. Get it.** Three readings coexist:

1. **Comprehension**: "get it" as the moment of understanding. The studio makes technology make sense.
2. **Acquisition**: "get it" as receiving and owning. You ask for a product, you get a product. Yours.
3. **Imperative**: "go get it" as a command to fetch and deliver. The name reads like a function call: `code.getIt()`.

There is also a gift hiding in reading 2: **GET is the primal verb of the web itself.** Every page that has ever loaded began with `GET /`. A web studio whose name contains the web's first verb is a one-in-a-thousand naming accident.

And the logo already tells a story: the mark is **C `</>` E**, a word with code literally embedded inside it, drawn in two gradient voices (teal and warm orange).

### Conceptual directions derived from the name

1. Clarity emerging from complexity (the "aha" made visual)
2. Request and response (you ask, it arrives)
3. Ownership and handover (the keys to a finished thing)
4. Assembly (parts becoming one product)
5. Translation (between human intent and machine precision)
6. The bracket as aperture (everything passes through `< >`)
7. Materialization (idea compiled into artifact)
8. Drafting and engineering craft (the drawing before the building)
9. Systems made legible (invisible structure made visible)
10. The confirmation ("Got it." as a promise of reliability)
11. Architecture in layers (foundation up to identity)
12. The function call (the site as an executable named CodeGetIt)

## Phase 2: Ten Creative Directions

### 1. The Assembly

- **Core idea:** CodeGetIt does not decorate pages, it assembles products. The site is art-directed as a master engineering drawing on which the studio's work is drawn, exploded into its four layers, annotated, and assembled in front of the visitor.
- **Visual metaphor:** An exploded axonometric figure: Foundation, Systems, Interface, Identity, stacked on one axis, labeled like a technical plate. Hairline leader lines, figure captions, an index of capabilities. The brand chevrons are the final component, lowered onto the top of the stack: identity completes the build.
- **Motion language:** Assembly choreography. Nothing fades in aimlessly; every element is *installed*: planes rise into position bottom-up, hairlines draw, labels register, the chevrons drop in last with weight. Hovering the figure pulls the explosion apart; leaving lets it settle. Remove the motion and you lose the entire narrative of "being built."
- **Emotional response:** "These people build properly." Calm confidence, precision, the pleasure of watching something click into place.
- **Relationship to brand:** Reading 4 + 8 + 11 of the name. It depicts building complete products without showing a single line of code, and it gives the logo a structural job.
- **Memorability: 9.** "The site where the product drawing assembles itself" survives 24 hours.
- **Risks:** Can drift cold/technical if annotation outweighs warmth; drafting hairlines can decay into decoration if not disciplined.

### 2. GET /

- **Core idea:** The whole site is one request-response ritual. Arriving fires `GET /`; every section is a request the page answers by delivering content into place.
- **Visual metaphor:** Request lines as typographic objects; payloads arriving; the moment of "200 OK" as a satisfying settle.
- **Motion language:** Ask, then receive. Elements arrive like deliveries along visible paths. Without motion the metaphor is dead.
- **Emotional response:** Insider wit for technical visitors; snappy reliability for everyone.
- **Relationship to brand:** The deepest name pun available; literally nobody else can own GET.
- **Memorability: 9** for developers, **5** for CodeGetIt's actual buyers (small businesses, non-technical founders) who do not know what GET is.
- **Risks:** Meaning gated behind protocol literacy; flirts with the banned terminal aesthetic.

### 3. The Aperture

- **Core idea:** Everything CodeGetIt makes passes between the two brackets of the logo. The chevron pair becomes a gate, a lens, a masking device through which all content is revealed.
- **Visual metaphor:** Chevron-shaped wipes and clips; images and sections revealed through an opening `< >`.
- **Motion language:** Open, pass through, close. Transitions are the identity.
- **Emotional response:** Sleek, cinematic, controlled.
- **Relationship to brand:** Derived 1:1 from the logo geometry.
- **Memorability: 7.** Strong as a device, thin as a worldview; a transition grammar is not a story.
- **Risks:** One-trick; without exceptional craft it reads as a fancy page-transition library.

### 4. Got It.

- **Core idea:** The site behaves like a counterpart who listens. Every interaction is acknowledged ("Got it."); the page responds to you the way the studio responds to clients.
- **Visual metaphor:** Conversational microcopy as a design layer; confirmations as typographic moments.
- **Emotional response:** Warm, human, trustworthy.
- **Memorability: 6.** Charming in the first minute, gimmick by the third.
- **Risks:** Cuteness fatigue; hard to keep out of chatbot territory.

### 5. The Prism

- **Core idea:** Ideas enter as white light and exit as spectrum. The logo's two gradients (teal, warm orange) are the refraction of one input into engineered output.
- **Visual metaphor:** Light planes splitting into the two brand gradients at section boundaries.
- **Emotional response:** Transformation, polish.
- **Memorability: 6.** Beautiful but dangerously close to the banned "generic gradient" family.
- **Risks:** One wrong move and it IS the gradient slop it tries to transcend.

### 6. Load-Bearing Type

- **Core idea:** Typography as structure. Syne headlines literally support, frame, and carry the page's elements, like beams and columns.
- **Visual metaphor:** Letters as architecture; content shelved on type.
- **Emotional response:** Boldness, structural wit.
- **Memorability: 7.**
- **Risks:** Hard to sustain across content-heavy sections; accessibility of text-as-object needs care.

### 7. The Index

- **Core idea:** The studio as meticulous registry. The whole site is one beautiful, obsessive catalog of capabilities, projects, and decisions.
- **Visual metaphor:** Editorial index tables, plates, cross-references.
- **Emotional response:** Rigor, curation.
- **Memorability: 6.** Already a known portfolio-site genre.
- **Risks:** Cold; low warmth for SMB buyers; was already evaluated and beaten in an earlier exploration round.

### 8. First Pixel

- **Core idea:** Every product begins as a single teal pixel. The site grows everything (layouts, sections, figures) by propagation from one seed point.
- **Visual metaphor:** One pixel that subdivides, multiplies, and blooms into systems.
- **Emotional response:** Origin, growth, care at the smallest scale ("designed to the pixel").
- **Memorability: 7.**
- **Risks:** Abstract; propagation animations are expensive to do well and cheap-looking when done badly.

### 9. The Handover

- **Core idea:** The defining moment of the studio relationship: receiving the finished thing. Everything on the site is staged as a delivery into your hands.
- **Visual metaphor:** Objects sliding in from off-canvas onto pedestals; the moment of possession.
- **Emotional response:** Ownership, completion.
- **Memorability: 5.** The feeling is right but the visual language is vague.
- **Risks:** No distinct image to remember; collapses into "things animate in."

### 10. Two Voices

- **Core idea:** Design speaks Syne, engineering speaks Outfit. The site is a duet between an expressive voice and a precise voice, in layout, motion, and copy.
- **Visual metaphor:** Split compositions where eccentric display type converses with engineered body text.
- **Emotional response:** Sophistication, duality mastered.
- **Memorability: 6.** Typographically rich but conceptually quiet; visitors feel it without being able to name it.
- **Risks:** Too subtle to survive text removal; depends entirely on execution finesse.

## Phase 3: Competitive Evaluation

| Direction | Original | Memorable | Premium | Brand fit | Stands out | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **The Assembly** | 8 | 9 | 9 | 9 | 9 | **44** |
| GET / | 10 | 7 | 7 | 9 | 9 | 42 |
| The Aperture | 7 | 7 | 8 | 9 | 7 | 38 |
| Load-Bearing Type | 8 | 7 | 7 | 6 | 8 | 36 |
| First Pixel | 7 | 7 | 7 | 7 | 7 | 35 |
| The Prism | 6 | 6 | 8 | 7 | 6 | 33 |
| Two Voices | 7 | 6 | 8 | 7 | 5 | 33 |
| The Index | 5 | 6 | 8 | 6 | 5 | 30 |
| Got It. | 7 | 6 | 5 | 8 | 6 | 32 |
| The Handover | 5 | 5 | 6 | 7 | 5 | 28 |

- **Most original:** GET /
- **Most memorable:** The Assembly
- **Most premium:** The Assembly
- **Most aligned with the brand:** The Assembly and GET / (tied on different readings of the name)
- **Most likely to stand out from competitors:** The Assembly

### The winner: The Assembly

Justification:

1. It is the only direction scoring top-two on every axis. GET / is more original but gates its meaning behind protocol literacy that CodeGetIt's actual buyers (small businesses, local brands, non-technical founders) do not have.
2. It passes the text-removal test: an exploded, annotated product figure assembling itself is recognizable and rememberable with every word deleted.
3. It communicates the commercial offer without a single cliché: "we build complete products, every layer, under one roof" is *depicted*, not claimed.
4. It gives the logo a structural role (the chevrons are the Identity layer that completes the build) instead of parking it in a corner.
5. It is native to the type system: Syne's geometric eccentricity reads as drafted lettering; Outfit reads as annotation.
6. It already beat "Blueprint" and "Index" in a previous evaluation round; this round it beat nine fresh challengers.

Two losing directions are absorbed as subordinate systems, both derived from the same logo so they cannot fracture the identity:

- **From The Aperture:** the chevron gate becomes the site's reveal grammar (image masks, section transitions).
- **From GET /:** the ask-then-receive principle becomes the motion law: nothing fades vaguely, everything is requested and delivered into position.

## Phase 4: Art Direction Document

### Story

You land on what feels like a drafting table, not a website. A four-line headline sets itself like type being locked into a chase: "Designed to the pixel, engineered to last." To its right, a technical figure assembles: four planes rise onto one axis (Foundation, then Systems, then Interface), hairlines draw out to their labels, and finally the two brand chevrons drop onto the top of the stack with the weight of a stamp: Identity. A caption registers underneath: *Fig. 01 · A product, assembled.* When you move your cursor over the figure, the explosion breathes apart, showing you its layers; when you leave, it settles. Below the fold, every section continues the language of the sheet: capabilities are an index, process steps are assembly stages, projects are plates. Twenty-four hours later you may not remember a word, but you remember the drawing that built itself.

### Layout system

- **The sheet, not the screen.** The page is composed like a technical plate: a working area with deliberate margins, content aligned to a 12-column grid, and one strong asymmetry per section.
- **Hero:** message left (6 columns), figure right (6 columns), capabilities index running under a single hairline rule across the full width. No centered compositions.
- **Sections below:** alternate full-width index layouts with asymmetric figure-plus-text plates. No layout family repeats more than once.
- **Hairlines are workers, not decoration.** A rule may appear only to attach a label to a thing or to separate real content groups.

### Visual hierarchy

1. **First:** the headline staircase (largest mass, highest contrast, animates first).
2. **Second:** the assembling figure (motion attracts the eye the moment the headline settles).
3. **Third:** the primary CTA (the only saturated teal surface on the screen).
4. **Fourth:** subtitle, then the capabilities index.

### Typography strategy

- **Syne (display):** ExtraBold, tight tracking (-0.025em), used exclusively for headlines and figure-scale numerals. The staircase indent (0.35em per line) is the typographic signature; it mirrors the figure's diagonal axis. Syne ExtraBold runs roughly 0.8em per character; headline copy is tuned to that scale constraint.
- **Outfit (body):** All annotation, body, labels, and UI. Labels at 11px with wide tracking exist only as figure annotation, never as section eyebrows.
- **Emphasis system:** within a Syne headline, one line may be stroke-outlined (drafted, not yet inked) and one line may be brand teal (the inked, finished line). This is the drawing-versus-built duality in a single headline.

### Motion choreography

- **Page load (the assembly sequence, about 1.6s total):** headline lines rise out of masks in sequence (120ms stagger) → subtitle and CTAs install → figure planes rise bottom-up (160ms stagger) → chevrons drop with a spring → leader lines draw, labels register → index rule draws, index entries tick in → caption stamps last.
- **Scroll:** sections are installed as they enter: rules draw, then content is delivered onto them. One choreographed reveal per section, no continuous scroll-jacking.
- **Micro-interactions:** the primary CTA is magnetic with an arrow chip; the figure explodes apart on hover and settles on leave; outlined headline lines ink themselves teal on hover.
- **Transitions:** chevron-gate wipes for route/image reveals (the absorbed Aperture system).
- **Depth:** built from stacking order and overlap (planes, masks), never from blur or glow.
- **Rhythm:** a shared easing curve, cubic-bezier(0.32, 0.72, 0, 1), and a single timeline constant govern every duration so the whole page feels conducted.
- **Idle life:** exactly one perpetual motion on the page: the chevrons hover almost imperceptibly above the stack. Identity floats; everything else stands still.
- **Reduced motion:** every choreography collapses to its final state instantly. The page is fully composed without a single animation.

### Logo integration

The logo is disassembled into working parts:

1. **The chevrons are a component of the product.** In the hero figure they are the Identity layer, physically lowered onto the stack. The brand does not sit above the page; it is installed into the work.
2. **The chevron gate** is the reveal mask for images and transitions site-wide.
3. **The full mark** appears conventionally only in the navbar and footer, in its two-gradient form.

### Mobile experience

Not a reduction, a re-staging: the sheet becomes a vertical scroll of plates. The headline staircase keeps its indents at a tuned mobile clamp. The figure moves below the CTAs at full width and keeps its complete assembly choreography (entrance is not gated to desktop); only the hover explosion is desktop-only, replaced by the settled exploded state. The capabilities index collapses to a 2x2 grid under the same drawn rule. Tap targets are full-row. Nothing is hidden; the order of reading is simply re-conducted: claim, proof, action, anatomy, index.

## Phase 5: Implementation Strategy

### Component architecture

```
src/components/landing/
  HeroSection.tsx          client; composition + index strip
  hero/
    HeroHeadline.tsx       staircase masked-rise headline (lines prop)
    HeroCtas.tsx           magnetic primary + ghost secondary
    AssemblyFigure.tsx     the exploded axonometric SVG figure
    heroMotion.ts          EASE + timeline T (single source of truth)
    hero.types.ts          HeroCopy derived from i18n Translations
```

- All motion components are client leaves; sections stay server-renderable where possible.
- Copy lives in `t.landing.hero` (`src/i18n/types.ts`); Greek aliases English.
- Retired and deleted: FlipWords, HeroShowcase, StatCounter, HeroBadge, HeroBrandMark, Spotlight, MorphingText (all pre-redesign patterns from the Do Not Use list).

### Motion architecture

- One easing (`EASE`) and one timeline object (`T`) in `heroMotion.ts`; every delay derives from it.
- Framer Motion only (project standard import: `framer-motion`); no GSAP, no scroll listeners.
- Continuous values (hover explosion) via MotionValues, never React state.
- `useReducedMotion()` checked at every animated leaf; reduced mode renders final states (`initial: false`).

### Performance

- The figure is a single inline SVG; all animation is transform/opacity/pathLength (GPU-composited, no layout).
- Hairline strokes use `vector-effect: non-scaling-stroke` so the isometric transform cannot fatten them.
- One perpetual animation (chevron float) on one small group; everything else runs once.
- No images in the hero: LCP is the headline text itself.

### Accessibility

- The figure is `aria-hidden` decoration with an `sr-only` description; layer names also exist as visible text in the index.
- Headline is one `h1`; masked lines remain real text (no splitting into letters).
- All interactive elements are real anchors with visible focus; contrast: slate-900 and slate-600 on `#fafafa`, white on brand-600, all AA or better.
- Hover-only effects (explosion, ink-fill) are enhancements; no information lives exclusively in them.
